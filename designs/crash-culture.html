A daily game to help connect with your cultural identity
Crash Culture is an interactive, educational trivia game that provides users with a gateway to learning more about their identity day-by-day. Each day users are provided with a singular multiple-choice trivia question about a topic within their ethnic culture from food to language and more. Beyond the game, users are encouraged to engage more deeply with their identity either via conversations with peers in their community or learning through digital resources.

Problem

A gap between the increasing population of second-generation immigrants and their heritage is growing and preventing them from connecting with the culture of their parents leading to dissonance between their cultural identity and their newfound American identity. Currently, there does not exist a centralized digital resource for children of immigrants to reliably learn about the varying aspects of their identities.

Solution

A daily game that helps second-generation immigrants approach cultural identity education at their own pace with curated resources and personalized interactions.

My Role

I went through an entire cycle of the design process over the course of a semester to produce a mobile app concept of my own idea from empathy to testing. At the end, I made recommendations on the potential success of the app based on user data.

Empathy & Define

I was charged with approaching the Empathy phase of this iteration of the design process with qualitative methods, particularly with interviews to help inform the building of Empathy Maps, User Personas, and Current and Future States.

I conducted 9 in-depth interviews with those in my network that identified as either second-generation immigrants or first-generation immigrants that immigrated at a young age. In these interviews, I learned the stories of how younger immigrants are navigating the tumultuous journey of being part of a marginalized population in America. These personas are a culmination of their experiences, struggles, needs and frustrations.

Persona 1: The need for flexibility

BIG TAKEAWAY
There is a population of young immigrants coasting through life while passively attending to their cultural identity who are seeking a way to learn more in a noncommittal, low stakes capacity due to their busy schedules.

Persona 2: The need for intimacy

BIG TAKEAWAY
There is a population of young immigrants that have grown up with stronger ties to their cultural identity and community due to circumstance who are seeking a means to stake more initiative in creating experiences for themselves that can deepen their connection to their heritage.

Persona 3: The need for comprehensiveness and reliability

BIG TAKEAWAY
There is a group of young immigrants that have been isolated because of a complete lack of access to any aspect of their culture who are seeking a starting point to dive into comprehensive, reliable resources.

Ideate

After conducting interviews and performing online research, I developed themes that corresponded to each of the personas that embodied different demographics from my research. All of the themes were inspired by diverse applications that had various functions that I believed would benefit the intended users.

Theme 1: Crash Culture
INSPIRED BY
Wordle (a daily word game) and BeReal (a social media based in organic content creation where users only post once-per-day)

WHAT I SAW
A need for a low-commitment solution that users would still be incentivized to engage with to receive bits of information about their culture that have the potential to lead to deeper engagements.
THE CONCEPT & GOAL

A daily trivia game about one's culture that uses "Lifelines" that nudge users to engage with their community. Crash Culture is a daily gateway for users to explore their culture and connect with other cultural community members digitally, utlizing user's pre-existing networks while also expanding them. I wanted to pull from the low-stakes commitment of a short game once per day while having the brief, yet meaningful engagement of BeReal. This way there would be a level of flexibility allowing users to choose how in-depth they'd like to immerse themselves while still being motivated to move past the scary first steps.
Theme 2: Family Ties
INSPIRED BY
Out of the Blue (an app that randomly generates contacts to keep in touch with via call or text)

WHAT I SAW
A group of young immigrants seeking deeper, intimate, and organic connections with those in their community.
THE CONCEPT & GOAL

Family Ties is a mobile app that sorts out users by demographic and locality then randomly generates groupchats and assigns relevant conversation prompts once per week. Users are then incentivized to remain connected with their weekly groupchats and even meet in person via a rewards system. I wanted to nudge users to connect with those in their cultural community that they come to like in a pseudo-groupchat-platonic-speeddating setting. With this, there would be opportunities for forming deep connections with those they may not have connected with in their community.
Theme 3: Historicultural
INSPIRED BY
Mimo (front-end development module-based education)

WHAT I SAW
A need for a centralized, reliable platform for cultural education that users could take at their own pace.
THE CONCEPT & GOAL

A module-based educational platform that works with users at their own speed to dive into the history of their cultural identity that they were deprived of while growing up. Historicultural is an educational ecosystem that rewards users for their daily engagement while tracking users via "nontraditional" benchmarking such as a simple conversation with a friend about a module's topic. With this concept, I wanted to provide an educational platform that users of any level could join while still incorporating a low level of community engagement. This concept is more information heavy for those that are less inclined to cold-contact those in their community.

Prototype

After a class-wide review of the themes I produced in the ideation stage, I took into account feedback and developed wireframes accordingly. The winning theme was Crash Culture, but I incorporated features from all three concepts because I saw that it benefitted the medium of the Crash Culture concept while also not distracting from the core value of gamifying cultural immersion.

The prototype has 5 main user flows: Onboarding, Question of the Day & Lifelines, Prompt of the Day, Friends Management, and Profile Management. All of these were made to support the goal of giving young immigrants the ability to immerse themselves in their culture and communities at their own pace while still being incentivized to return day after day.

The Question of the Day helps users learn baseline, surface-level information to help them dip their toes in (Persona 1 & 3). The Lifelines nudge users to engage with the greater digital community of their culture (Persona 2). The Prompt of the Day allows for deeper connection via storytelling and discussion (Persona 2). Lastly, the additional resources found in Profile Management, gives users the base they need to dive deeper into more education (Persona 3).

Full Interactive Prototype

The goal of the prototype was to provide an immersive experience of the main function of Crash Culture: gamifying cultural immersion and education. I limited the user flows to remain mostly constrained so that test users would move through the prototype in a way that aligned with my testing protocol so that I could collect accurate data on specific user flows.

Test

I tested the Adobe XD prototype with five users who fit the testing requirements: young immigrants with access to technology. To do this, I created testing prototcol and conducted the tests in person, recording results and getting feedback in real time. I broke down the prototype into 6 different tasks. Based on the results of the test, I compiled my findings, made recommendations, and concluded on whether the application could prove to be successful in implementation.

Conclusion & Recommendations

The results of the user tests proved that the Crash Culture concept could be successful if implemented with the following recommendations:
RECOMMENDATIONS
  • Increase the visibility of the information regarding Lifelines to ensure users understand Lifelines in their totality before engaging in them.
  • Increase the visual cues in the context of expanding the posts of other users in the “Community” section.
  • Improving the navigation of the Calendar Archive feature within the “Profile” section so that users can reliably engage with its full functionality.
  • Add mores color for emphasis and overall aesthetics.
  • Test the navigation between sections in a fully funtional prototype.

Key Takeaways and Lessons Learned

Overall, this project is the one on my portfolio I've derived the most value from on a personal level. Because it was an opportunity to pour my identity and passions into, I found myself working at it more dilligently and carefully than anything in the past. Crash Culture has taught me what project ownership can look like and how rewarding it can be. I was able to make my own decisions from empathy to testing and everything in between and implement ideas that were very representative of who I am as a person. Beyond the personal impact, I've developed new skills in Qualitative Research and Testing that I wasn't able to fully explore in my other portfolio projects due to constraints.

CHALLENGES

Because this project was so near and dear to my heart, I had to continuously remind myself to remain objective in my evaluation and synthesis of data collected at both the Empathy and Testing stages. I found myself getting lost in what the solution could look like as opposed to what my persona's needs were. To remedy this, I continously referred to exact quotes from interviews and asked for outside opinions to ensure that my work was still benefitting my intended user. Building out my user personas was essential to this process since they helped me prevent myself from falling into the Self-Referential design pitfall. I continuously adjusted my design process so that I could go back and check that my design decisions were aligning with the problems expressed in the data I collected to remain impartial.

WHAT I WOULD DO DIFFERENTLY

To make this a more successful product, I would have taken more time to make the features more comprehensive in the interactive prototype prior to testing due to the fact that I felt that its limitations were preventing test users from immersing themselves in the experience I intended. This way, I would've been able to collect better data that would allow me to iterate the interactive prototype more efficiently. In addition, I would conducted more testing overall. A sample size of five users isn't enough data to make proper recommendations, so I would adapt my protocol to make the testing more accessible remotely so that I could get a larger sample size.

HOW I HELPED

My entire life I have been lost on how exactly to navigate the dichotomy between the Vietnamese culture I was born from and the American culture I was raised in. For so long I didn't know how important it was to be able to learn and retain the rich culture of my heritage, and a big problem was a lack of acceess to tools that could help me find a starting point. It wasn't until I sought out resources myself that I realizeded how widespread the issue is and how common my story was. Seeing how every person that tested my prototype was ecstatic asbout the idea of using it in its finished form and that they wished they had something like it sooner, made me realize how important this is. My plan is to actually build out Crash Culture so that future young immigrants will have a tool to explore their culture in exactly the way they want.
